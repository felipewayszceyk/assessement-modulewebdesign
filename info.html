Why Ireland?

Moving to Ireland:

Real Costs, Visa, Jobs & What they do not tell

Moving to Ireland is something a lot of people from Brazil and other countries are thinking about, and honestly, it makes sense. One of the biggest reasons is the quality of life things just feel safer and more organized. I also like the fact that Ireland has a lot of job opportunities, especially with big international companies based here. Another huge plus is being able to improve your English just by living your daily life. If you come as a student, you can also work, which helps a lot financially. People here are generally friendly, which makes adapting easier. The location is great too, since you can travel around Europe without much hassle. Salaries are better compared to many other countries, which helps if you're trying to save money. If you have a European passport or a different visa, things can be even easier. In the end, it’s not just about work it’s a life experience that really helps you grow.

Why did we choose Ireland again?

After spending some time reflecting on our journey, me and my girlfriend decided to go back to Ireland because it feels like the right place for both of us to grow again. I’m currently going through a career transition into software development, and being in Ireland gives me access to more opportunities in the tech industry and a stronger professional environment. For both of us, it also means stability, better chances to build our future, and continue improving our English in a daily immersive way. We already know the country, so it feels less like starting over and more like continuing a path we already began. On top of that, Ireland offers a good balance between work and quality of life, which is important for the plans we have together.

Ways to Move to Ireland

For non-European citizens, there are a few main ways to move to Ireland. One of the most common is the student visa, which allows people to study English or higher education courses and also work part-time during their stay. Another option is the employment permit system, like the Critical Skills Employment Permit, which is aimed at people working in high-demand areas such as tech, and the General Employment Permit for other qualified jobs. There is also the Working Holiday Visa for certain nationalities, which allows young people to live and work in Ireland for a limited time. Some people also come through family reunification visas depending on their situation.
For European citizens, the process is much simpler because of the freedom of movement within the EU. They don’t need a visa or work permit to live, work, or study in Ireland, which makes relocating much easier and faster. In practice, they can just arrive and start building their life without the same immigration barriers.

To be Prepared

Moving to Ireland takes more than just the desire to live abroad, it really requires planning and preparation. One of the most important things is financial preparation, since the cost of living, especially rent, can be quite high in the first few months. It’s also essential to understand your visa situation, whether you’re coming as a student, with a work permit, or with European citizenship, because each option changes how you need to plan your move. English preparation is another key point, even if you plan to improve it once you arrive, having a basic level before coming makes adaptation and job searching much easier. It’s also important to research the country in advance, learning how transport, healthcare, and the job market actually work. Many people underestimate how difficult it can be to find accommodation, so having temporary housing planned is very important. Having some savings for unexpected situations also makes a big difference during the transition. In general, the more prepared you are before arriving, the smoother your adaptation process will be and the easier it is to deal with the challenges at the beginning.

💰 Financial Preparation

Moving to Ireland requires planning. The cost of living, especially rent, is high, so it’s important to arrive with some financial backup to avoid stress in the first months.

🏠 Finding Accommodation

One of the biggest challenges in Ireland is housing. It can take time to find a place, and competition is high, especially in Dublin. Being prepared for this makes a huge difference.

📚 Understand the Country

Before moving, it’s important to learn how things work here — from public transport to banking, healthcare, and work culture. It helps you adapt much faster.

💼 Job Market Reality

Ireland has good job opportunities, especially in certain areas like tech and services, but getting your first job can take time depending on your experience and English level.

🌍 Expectations vs Reality

Life in Ireland is great in many ways, but it’s not perfect. Weather, rent prices, and housing can be challenging, but safety and opportunities balance a lot of it.

🧠 What I’ve learned so far

Living in another country teaches you a lot — not just about work or language, but about yourself, your limits, and what really matters.
